## Top Stories

### 1. US-IRAN CEASEFIRE — MIDDLE EAST

**What happened**

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated airstrikes on Iran targeting nuclear infrastructure, ballistic missile facilities, and naval assets [S1]. Iran retaliated by striking Israeli and US positions and effectively closing the Strait of Hormuz [S1][S6]. On April 7, the US and Iran announced a two-week ceasefire: Iran agreed to reopen the Strait; the US agreed to suspend strikes; peace talks were scheduled in Islamabad [S1][S2]. Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu's office stated that it supports Trump's decision, but that the ceasefire explicitly does not cover Lebanon [S2]. The death toll in the Lebanon-Hezbollah war has surpassed 1,600 [S2]. Iran has since accused the United States of violating multiple clauses of the ceasefire framework [S3]. Russia and China vetoed a UN Security Council resolution concerning the Strait of Hormuz [S4].

> "The ceasefire has been obtained under the threat of a massive attack against Iran's civilian infrastructure." — Chatham House experts [S7]

**Expert analysis**

Chatham House analysts describe the ceasefire as structurally unstable: Israel remains deeply sceptical of any arrangement that leaves Iran's missile, nuclear, and regional capabilities intact, and is prepared to continue operations in Lebanon regardless [S7]. The CFR notes that many key factors determining durability remain unclear [S8]. CSIS identifies the primary flashpoints to watch: the Lebanon carve-out, Iran's missile programme, and the absence of any agreed mechanism for verification [S9].

**Structural analysis**

The war's trigger — Iran's effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz — is best understood not as a military retaliation but as a strike at the structural foundation of US global power: the petrodollar system. Since 1973, global oil pricing in US dollars has underwritten American financial hegemony. Iran, reportedly with Chinese backing, began conditioning tanker passage on yuan-denominated payment [S5][S20][S21]. Al Jazeera's economic analysis frames this explicitly: Iran and China are using the Hormuz crisis as a lever against dollar hegemony [S5]. The Dallas Fed estimates that continued Strait closure — blocking roughly 20% of global oil supply — would push WTI oil to $98 per barrel and cut global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points annualised [S6].

The US threat to seize Kharg Island, Iran's primary oil export terminal, reveals the material logic beneath the stated justification of nuclear non-proliferation: control of energy infrastructure [S2]. The ceasefire does not resolve this. It suspends it for two weeks.

**Philosophical framing**

*David Harvey — Accumulation by Dispossession*

Harvey's framework in *The New Imperialism* (2003) provides the most precise analytical lens for this conflict. Harvey argues that when overaccumulated capital faces a crisis of surplus absorption, imperial states resort to coercive dispossession of externally held resources — land, labour, natural assets — as a "spatial fix." The US-Israel strikes on Iran's nuclear and naval assets are not a departure from this logic but its expression: the goal is not the destruction of Iran per se but the restoration of an energy geography in which oil flows freely, denominated in dollars, underwriting American financial solvency. The ceasefire, from this perspective, is not peace — it is a two-week options position.

**Historical checkpoint**

*1953 Operation Ajax — the Mossadegh coup*

In 1951, Iranian Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh nationalised the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC — now BP), seeking to audit royalties and assert sovereign control over Iranian oil reserves. The UK and US, alarmed by the precedent, orchestrated his overthrow in August 1953 via the CIA's Operation Ajax and MI6's Operation Boot. The Shah was reinstalled; oil contracts were reassigned to Western companies. The structural parallel to 2026 is direct: then as now, the trigger for US intervention was a threat to Western control of Iranian energy infrastructure. The 1979 Islamic Revolution was in large part a delayed consequence of that coup. What consequences the 2026 war will produce, a generation hence, remains to be seen.

### 2. US-CHINA TARIFF WAR — GLOBAL ECONOMY

**What happened**

US tariffs on Chinese goods reached 145% on certain product categories; China responded with retaliatory tariffs of up to 125% on US products [S12][S13]. By mid-2025, US imports from China had fallen to roughly half their year-prior levels — lows not seen since the 2009 financial crisis [S13]. China twice in 2025 threatened to cut off US automobile manufacturers' access to essential Chinese inputs [S12]. In February 2026, the US Supreme Court struck down many of Trump's 2025 tariffs, forcing a recalibration; new Section 301 investigations were opened against China, Vietnam, Taiwan, Mexico, Japan, and the EU in March [S13]. Trump has announced a planned April 2026 summit with President Xi Jinping in Beijing to discuss trade, military issues, and bilateral ties [S14]. The summit signals a desire for de-escalation, though structural contradictions remain unresolved [S15].

**Expert analysis**

Foreign Affairs publishes a blunt structural assessment: China has escalation dominance in this trade war. The United States imports goods from China that cannot be replaced quickly or made domestically at anything less than prohibitive cost [S10]. PIIE analysis shows that after a 30% drop in US-China trade, the US replaced roughly two-thirds of the gap with imports from other markets, while Chinese exporters cut prices by an average of 8% to find new buyers — suggesting the pain asymmetry runs against Washington [S11]. CFR notes that Trump's negotiating truce has come at a cost to US credibility with allies who restructured their own trade postures in response to signals that ultimately proved reversible [S15].

**Structural analysis**

The trade war is misrepresented in mainstream coverage as a dispute over deficits and intellectual property. Its structural significance is the contest over who controls the production architecture of 21st-century capitalism. China remains the centre of global manufacturing — the US the primary consumer market. Tariffs cannot rebalance this relationship; they can only impose cost on it. The Supreme Court's intervention reveals a further structural contradiction: the US executive's attempt to unilaterally weaponise trade policy runs against the constitutional architecture of American governance itself, creating legal and diplomatic unpredictability that compounds the economic disruption. The April summit is an acknowledgement of structural limits, not a resolution of them.

**Postcolonial lens**

The framing of China as a "rule-breaker" in international trade ignores the colonial genealogy of those rules. The WTO system was constructed in the 1990s under conditions of US hegemony, structuring global trade in ways that favoured deindustrialised Global North markets as consumers and Global South economies as low-cost producers. China's industrial policy — condemned as "subsidy cheating" — mirrors the protectionist strategies that the US, Germany, and Japan used in their own industrialisation phases. The trade war is, in part, a structural response by the former rule-maker to a rising power that learned to play the game too well.

### 3. RUSSIA CLAIMS LUHANSK — UKRAINE

**What happened**

Russia's Ministry of Defence announced that forces have taken full control of the Luhansk region in eastern Ukraine, describing it as the "liberation of the Luhansk People's Republic" [S17]. Over the period April 2025 to April 2026, Russian forces gained approximately 1,897 square miles of Ukrainian territory [S17 via Russia Matters data]. Trump and Zelenskyy have reportedly agreed on 90–95% of a peace proposal framework [S17]. Under the emerging proposal framework, Crimea, Luhansk, and Donetsk would be recognised as de facto Russian by the United States [S19].

**Expert analysis**

Atlantic Council analysis states that Putin cannot accept any peace deal that secures Ukrainian statehood, as any such settlement would be interpreted in Moscow as a historic Russian defeat [S18]. CSIS assesses the current peace deal proposal as structurally problematic: a framework in which Ukraine cedes territory it does not legally recognise as Russian, without ironclad security guarantees, constitutes a strategic trap rather than a durable settlement [S19]. The Kremlin has remained largely absent from recent multilateral peace talks involving the US, Ukraine, and European officials [S18].

**Structural analysis**

The peace deal framework — if enacted — would formalise a territorial settlement that rewards four years of attritional warfare at the cost of international legal norms against conquest. The structural significance extends beyond Ukraine: it would set a precedent that territorial acquisition through sustained military violence can eventually be legitimised by great-power diplomacy. The NATO alliance faces its most significant test of post-Cold War credibility. European members face a choice between accepting a US-brokered settlement that weakens their eastern frontier or financing Ukrainian resistance independently of Washington.

## Under-Reported Story

**WTO Ministerial Collapse in Cameroon**

At the WTO Ministerial conference held in Cameroon this week, the world's major trading blocs — including the EU, UK, and Canada — effectively abandoned collective multilateral decision-making, retreating to voluntary and plurilateral agreements among themselves. This is a structural inflection point: the rules-based multilateral trading order, already under strain since 2017, has now been formally hollowed out by its own architects. The implication for Global South economies is severe: smaller nations lose the only institutional forum in which they could theoretically constrain great-power trade behaviour. This development received almost no coverage in Western outlets consumed with the ceasefire and tariff headlines.
